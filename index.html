Vidēvdād

Of the three major divisions of the 21 Nasks of the Sasanian Avesta, the Vendīdād—Avestan widaēwa-dāta-, Pahl. jud-dēw-dād “The Law repudiating the Demons” (Benveniste, 1970)—was the last of those called dādīg “dealing with law,” and 19th overall. The summary of its contents given in the 9th-century Dēnkard (Bk. 8.44; West, 1892, pp. 6-8, 152-66; ed. Madan, pp. 777-84) accords closely with the extent of the received text. In both the latter and the former chapter 12 is omitted, though in the Mss the numbering after 11 jumps to 13. K2 and the Mss descended from it contain a chapter 12, but this is an 18th century composition (West, 1892, pp. 160-61). Thus, the received text of both the Avestan and Pahlavi versions (see below) is as it was in the 9th century, and we can only assume that at some point after the fall of the Sasanian empire the twelfth chapter became lost. Since this chapter is also absent from the Vendīdād Sāde Mss. (on which, see below), we can conclude that the composite texts were constructed after the 7th century, that is, after the loss of Chap. 12, and that the Widēwdād-ceremony (Modi, 1922, pp. 350-51) is an innovation of the Islamic period.

Dādestān ī Dēnīg

Content. Dādestān ī dēnīg is a strictly orthodox and traditional work, dealing with a variety of subjects ranging from religious to social, ethical, legal, and philosophical questions, as well as cosmology, natural phenomena, and the like.

Wizīdagīhā ī Zādspram

The book is both a good witness to Mazdean cosmogony and thought, but the author also seems to have drawn extensively on what had become, over the centuries of the Sassanid monarchy, a legacy in the Iranian world, namely the data of Greek philosophy, gnosis, Manichaeism in particular, but also Gre co-Chaldean medicine or astrology, not to mention influences from India or further east. While references to the Avesta and the Zand are obviously present in our text, but often difficult to identify, its main interest lies in the closely dependent conceptions of creation and the end of the world, in the analysis of the human compound, in anci ent Iranian mythology, and finally in the legends about Zoroaster, which are probably the result of late developments,7 because Mazdaism, like the other great religions, could not do without its own hagiography.

Evil Principles in Selected Middle Persian and Avestan Texts

While some Pahlavi texts focus more on Ahreman, or "Gannāg-Mēnōy," as the ultimate evil principle, Druz also plays an active role in other Middle Persian works such as Dādestān ī Dēnīg. This project combines comparative and digital humanities methods to examine how these concepts relate to each other, how they differ in Dādestān ī Dēnīg and Wizīdagīhā ī Zādspram, and what these principles may have meant to Mazdayasnian high priests in ninth-century Iran.
